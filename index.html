On Group Buys

A note on terminology:

When I hear "group buys," I think of true bulk orders — schools ordering 20-50 pieces at once, organizations placing wholesale-style orders.

What you're describing sounds more like multi-unit orders — customers buying 2-4+ items in a single purchase.

The good news: multi-unit orders are simpler to enable. No special infrastructure, no MOQs, no B2B fulfillment workflows.

Here's a distinction we've observed:

Group buys don't happen because you want them to.
They happen because customers want them to.

Most brands try to manufacture group-buy behavior through CRO tactics.

These tactics optimize for a behavior the customer hasn't decided to do yet.

Group buys happen when a customer naturally concludes: "I should get this for my whole team."

It comes from seeing four teachers wearing the same sweatshirt in your ad and thinking: "That's us."

You need three levers working together: Offer, Creative, and Design.

Lever 1: Offer

Quantity discounts tend to work well from what we've seen.

Example here that works with your margins if you bump up prices (hence why unit economics are tied to getting your multi-unit order volume up):

Quantity Discount
Buy 2 5% off
Buy 3 10% off
Buy 4+ 15% off

Lever 2: Creative

Add creative showing multiple people wearing the product together into the mix.

Examples:

  • Four teachers in a classroom wearing matching sweatshirts
  • A therapy team in coordinated colors
  • A family of four at an autism walk

This plants the idea: "This is something groups buy together."

It allows Meta to ingest the context that the product is meant for groups, and will serve and seek out consumers who are in the consideration stage of buying group based apparel.

AI image generation makes this easier than ever — you don't need to coordinate a photoshoot with 4+ people. I can show you examples if helpful.

Lever 3: Design

Some designs are inherently "individual" — they make sense worn alone.

Some designs are inherently "collective" — they naturally lend themselves to being worn as a group.

Collective Design Examples:

  • "Our Classroom Celebrates Neurodiversity"
  • "We See Every Mind"
  • "We Know Different Isn't Deficit"
  • "This Family Knows Different Is Beautiful"

On Bundles

You mentioned bundles haven't gotten traction historically.

That's not surprising.

Bundles work when there's:

  • Natural consumption pairing (shampoo + conditioner)
  • A discovery problem (starter kit for a new hobby)
  • Gift-giving convenience

Apparel is browsing behavior.

People want to pick what they want, not buy pre-packaged sets.

Forcing bundles is fighting human behavior.

I'd skip this as a priority.

A Recap — What I'd Focus On For Q1 2026 If I Was In Your Shoes

  1. Raise prices before AAM Pick one of the two paths modeled above. Even the conservative option gets you from 46% to 51% gross margin.
  2. Add quantity discounts Buy 2 (5% off), Buy 3 (10% off), Buy 4+ (15% off). Rewards the multi-unit behavior that's already happening.
  3. Add group creative into the mix Teachers in classrooms. Families at walks. Therapy teams. AI makes multi-person creative easy to produce — no coordinating photoshoots. Let Meta find the group-buy customers.
  4. Launch new collections that lend themselves to group buys and life moments Think: classroom teams, family walks, therapy center staff. Test before AAM, lean into winners during April.
